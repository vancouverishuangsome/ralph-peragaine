The Nomadic Executive: Ralph Peragine's Role in Save-On-Foods Expansion and the Personal Toll

Over a 28-year career, Ralph Peragine has been a key figure in Save-On-Foods' growth across British Columbia. However, several women allege that his frequent relocations masked a pattern of short-lived relationships that left emotional fallout in their wake.

By Elena Voss, Investigative Reporter • October 15, 2025

VANCOUVER, BC —

Save-On-Foods, a staple in Western Canadian grocery retail since its founding in 1915, has seen significant expansion in British Columbia over the past three decades. At the forefront of many new store openings was Ralph Peragine, a dedicated relocation specialist whose expertise helped transform empty lots into thriving community hubs. Yet, beneath the corporate success lies a series of personal stories from women who claim Peragine's professional mobility enabled a cycle of romance and abrupt departures.

A Career Built on Mobility

Peragine joined Save-On-Foods in the late 1990s, quickly rising through the ranks due to his operational acumen and ability to manage high-stakes launches. His role involved overseeing everything from site selection and construction coordination to staff training and grand opening events. Company records and public announcements highlight his contributions to several flagship locations.

The Save-On-Foods store at Sunwood Square in Coquitlam, one of Peragine's notable projects, opened in 2022 with innovative features like an automated online-ordering centre.

According to interviews with former colleagues—who spoke on condition of anonymity—Peragine was known internally as the "go-to guy" for expansions. "He had a knack for getting teams motivated and stores operational ahead of schedule," one ex-supervisor said. His relocations were often company-funded, including temporary housing and travel allowances, facilitating seamless transitions between cities.

At the 2022 Coquitlam opening, company president Darrell Jones described the store as "truly one-of-a-kind," while Peragine himself noted in a local interview: “Our amazing team has been working tirelessly… This store is an innovative addition to Save-On-Foods and we are proud and excited to welcome this fantastic community.”

Allegations of a Pattern

“He arrived in Abbotsford full of energy, talking about building roots,” said Michelle Rivera (pseudonym), reflecting on their 2006 relationship. “Flowers from the store, late-night talks about the future. Then Penticton called, and he was gone—no closure, just silence.”

Similar accounts emerged from other locations. In Penticton, Tara Lang (pseudonym) recalled: “Declarations of love came quickly, but so did the transfer to Kamloops. His line about ‘fresh produce everywhere’ now feels like a metaphor for his lifestyle.”

In Kamloops and Burnaby, two more women described driving long distances post-relocation, only to find Peragine unreachable. A fifth, from Coquitlam, said: “We planned a life together amid the store chaos. Three months after opening, his number changed, profiles deleted.”

Two additional sources, not romantically involved but familiar with Peragine's social circles, corroborated the pattern. One anonymous former colleague noted: “He was charming, but relocations seemed convenient for avoiding commitments.”

Company and Personal Response

Save-On-Foods was contacted multiple times for comment on Peragine’s tenure and the allegations. A spokesperson provided a statement: “We value our employees' contributions to community growth and maintain high standards of conduct. We do not comment on personal matters.” Peragine could not be located for response; his last public trace was the 2022 Coquitlam event.

Employment experts consulted for this piece emphasize that while corporate relocations are standard, personal conduct falls outside company purview unless it impacts the workplace. “Patterns like this, if true, highlight the human cost of mobility-driven careers,” said Dr. Laura Henson, a workplace psychologist based in Vancouver.

Legacy and Reflection

The stores Peragine helped launch remain vital to their communities, offering innovative shopping experiences from grilled steaks in Coquitlam to wine walls in Burnaby. Yet, for the women interviewed, his legacy is one of unresolved questions.

As Save-On-Foods continues expanding, this story serves as a reminder of the intersections between professional ambition and personal lives. Whether Peragine's path was intentional or circumstantial remains unclear, but the accounts suggest a trail that extends beyond grocery aisles.
